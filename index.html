Article 19(1)(a)

Introduction & Constitutional Basis

Constitutional Foundation

Article 19(1)(a) - Freedom of Speech and Expression

"All citizens shall have the right to freedom of speech and expression"

RTI Connection: Right to receive information is integral to freedom of speech and expression

Historical Development

  • Pre-2005: No comprehensive law on information access
  • S.P. Gupta Case (1981): First judicial recognition of right to information
  • State of U.P. v. Raj Narain (1975): "Right to know" precedent established
  • 2005: Right to Information Act enacted by Parliament
  • Constitutional Status: Recognized as fundamental right under Article 19(1)(a)

Administrative Law Principles

Transparency

Open governance and public access to information

Accountability

Public authorities answerable to citizens

Good Governance

Efficient and responsible public administration

RTI Act 2005

Right to Information Act, 2005

The Right to Information Act, 2005 (Act No. 22 of 2005)

Enactment: June 15, 2005

Enforcement: October 12, 2005

Application: Whole of India (except J&K - until 2019)

Objective: Promote transparency and accountability in governance

Key Definitions (Section 2)

Public Authority

Any authority or body established by Constitution, Parliament, State Legislature, or by government notification

Information

Any material in any form including records, documents, memos, emails, opinions, advices, press releases, circulars, orders, logbooks, contracts, reports, papers, samples, models, data material held in electronic form

Right to Information

Right to information accessible under this Act which is held by or under the control of any public authority

Objectives of RTI Act

  • Promote transparency in public administration
  • Make government more accountable to people
  • Reduce corruption in public offices
  • Enable informed participation in democratic process
  • Improve efficiency of public services

Scope and Coverage

  • All Central and State Government departments
  • Constitutional bodies and authorities
  • Local self-government institutions
  • NGOs substantially financed by government
  • Public sector undertakings
Sections 12-18

Institutional Framework

Information Commissions Structure

Central Level

Central Information Commission (CIC)

Composition: Chief Information Commissioner + up to 10 Information Commissioners

Appointment: President on recommendation of committee comprising PM, Leader of Opposition, Union Minister

Tenure: 5 years or 65 years of age, whichever is earlier

Status: Same as Election Commissioner

State Level

State Information Commission (SIC)

Composition: State Chief Information Commissioner + up to 10 State Information Commissioners

Appointment: Governor on recommendation of committee comprising CM, Leader of Opposition, State Minister

Tenure: 5 years or 65 years of age, whichever is earlier

Status: Same as State Election Commissioner

Information Officers

Public Information Officer (PIO)
  • Designated by public authority
  • Receive and respond to RTI applications
  • Assist applicants in seeking information
  • Transfer applications if required
Assistant Public Information Officer (APIO)
  • Receive RTI applications
  • Forward to PIO immediately
  • First point of contact for applicants
  • Facilitate information access

Powers and Functions (Section 18)

Adjudicatory Powers
  • Hear appeals and complaints
  • Order disclosure of information
  • Impose penalties on defaulting officers
  • Direct compliance with RTI provisions
Civil Court Powers
  • Summon witnesses and documents
  • Examine witnesses on oath
  • Discover and produce documents
  • Requisition public records
Sections 6-7

Key Provisions & Procedure

Section 3: Right to Information

"Subject to the provisions of this Act, all citizens shall have the right to information"

Scope of Right:

  • Right to information held by public authorities
  • Right to inspect documents and records
  • Right to take notes, extracts or certified copies
  • Right to take certified samples of material
  • Right to obtain information in electronic format

Section 4: Obligations of Public Authorities

"Every public authority shall maintain all its records duly catalogued and indexed"

Proactive Disclosure Requirements:

  • Organization structure and functions
  • Powers and duties of officers
  • Procedure followed in decision making
  • Norms set for discharge of functions
  • Rules, regulations, instructions, manuals
  • Categories of documents held
  • Directory of officers and employees
  • Monthly remuneration of officers
  • Budget allocated and manner of utilization
  • Manner of execution of subsidy programs
  • Particulars of recipients of concessions
  • Details of information available to public
  • Facilities available for obtaining information
  • Names, designations and contact details of PIOs
  • Any other information prescribed

RTI Application Procedure

Step 1: Application (Section 6)

  • Written application to PIO or APIO
  • Specify information sought
  • Pay prescribed fees (‚Çπ10 for general category)
  • No specific format required
  • Oral application for BPL applicants

Step 2: Response (Section 7)

  • Normal cases: 30 days from receipt
  • Life/Liberty matters: 48 hours
  • Third party involvement: Additional 5 days
  • PIO must provide information or reasons for denial
  • Transfer to concerned PIO if required

Step 3: Appeal Process (Section 19)

  • First Appeal: Within 30 days to First Appellate Authority
  • Second Appeal: Within 90 days to Information Commission
  • Appeal can be made for denial, delay, or unsatisfactory response
  • Information Commission has final authority
Sections 8-9

Exemptions & Limitations

Section 8: Exemption from Disclosure

8(1)(a) - National Security

Information affecting sovereignty and integrity of India, security, strategic, scientific or economic interests, relation with foreign State

8(1)(b) - Parliamentary Privilege

Information specifically forbidden to be published by Parliament or State Legislature

8(1)(c) - Court Orders

Information disclosure of which would constitute contempt of court

8(1)(d) - Commercial Confidence

Trade secrets, intellectual property, commercial confidence which would harm competitive position

8(1)(e) - Fiduciary Relationship

Information available to person in fiduciary relationship unless competent authority permits

8(1)(f) - Life and Safety

Information received in confidence from foreign government

8(1)(g) - Informant Protection

Information endangering life or physical safety of any person or identifying informant

8(1)(h) - Investigation Process

Information which would impede investigation process or prosecution of offenders

8(1)(i) - Cabinet Papers

Information relating to cabinet papers including records of deliberations of Council of Ministers

8(1)(j) - Personal Information

Personal information disclosure of which has no relationship to public activity or interest or would cause invasion of privacy

Section 9: Grounds for Rejection

Cabinet Papers Protection

Information relating to Cabinet papers, including records of deliberations of Council of Ministers, Secretaries and other officers

Exception: Information may be provided after decision is taken and matter is complete or 20 years have elapsed

Public Interest Override

Section 8(2) - Public Interest Test

Information shall be disclosed if public interest in disclosure outweighs the harm to protected interest

Factors for Public Interest:
  • Exposure of corruption or misconduct
  • Public health and safety concerns
  • Environmental protection
  • Ensuring accountability of public authorities
  • Promoting transparency in governance
Case Law

Landmark Supreme Court Cases

Foundational Judgments

S.P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981)

AIR 1982 SC 149
Principle: First judicial recognition of right to information as fundamental right under Article 19(1)(a)
Significance: "Government of an open society must be informed government. Democracy requires a citizenry that is informed about government's activities."

State of U.P. v. Raj Narain (1975)

AIR 1975 SC 865
Principle: Right to know is basic to democratic way of life and part of freedom of speech and expression
Significance: Established constitutional foundation for transparency in governance

Implementation and Interpretation Cases

Central Information Commissioner v. State of Manipur (2012)

AIR 2012 SC 289
Principle: Information Commission has power to impose penalty under Section 20
Significance: Clarified enforcement powers of Information Commissions

Institute of Chartered Accountants v. Shaunak Satya (2021)

2021 SCC OnLine SC 386
Principle: Professional bodies substantially financed by government are covered under RTI Act
Significance: Extended RTI coverage to professional regulatory bodies

Subhash Chandra Agarwal v. Indian National Congress (2013)

2020 5 SCC 481
Principle: Political parties performing public functions and receiving government funding are public authorities
Significance: Brought political parties under RTI ambit for transparency in funding

CBSE v. Aditya Bandopadhyay (2011)

AIR 2011 SC 3827
Principle: RTI cannot be used as tool of surveillance; information affecting privacy of students protected
Significance: Balanced right to information with privacy concerns in educational context

Recent Developments

RTI Amendment Act, 2019

Changes: Central Government to decide tenure and salary of Information Commissioners

Criticism: Concerns about independence of Information Commissions

COVID-19 and RTI

Impact: Extension of time limits due to pandemic

Digital Filing: Increased emphasis on online RTI applications

Modern Challenges

Implementation Challenges & Reforms

Administrative Barriers

Bureaucratic Resistance

Issues:
  • Unwillingness to share information
  • Culture of secrecy in government
  • Lack of training for PIOs
  • Inadequate infrastructure for information management
Solutions:
  • Regular training programs for officials
  • Performance evaluation linked to RTI compliance
  • Digital record management systems
  • Transparency awards and recognition

Awareness and Accessibility

Issues:
  • Low awareness among citizens
  • Language barriers in rural areas
  • Complex procedures for common people
  • Limited access to information in local languages
Solutions:
  • Public awareness campaigns
  • Simplification of application procedures
  • Regional language support
  • Community facilitation centers

Institutional Capacity

Issues:
  • Understaffed Information Commissions
  • Long pendency of appeals
  • Inadequate enforcement mechanisms
  • Limited resources for implementation
Solutions:
  • Strengthening Information Commissions
  • Time-bound disposal of cases
  • Enhanced penalty mechanisms
  • Adequate budget allocation

Reform Initiatives and Best Practices

Digital Transformation

  • Online RTI Portal: Central and state government web portals for RTI applications
  • Digital Records: Computerization of government records and documents
  • E-Governance: Integration with broader e-governance initiatives
  • Mobile Applications: Smartphone apps for RTI filing and tracking

Proactive Disclosure

  • Mandatory Publication: Regular publication of information under Section 4
  • Data Portals: Open government data initiatives
  • Citizen Charters: Clear service delivery standards
  • Suo Moto Disclosure: Voluntary disclosure beyond legal requirements

Capacity Building

  • Training Programs: Regular training for PIOs and government officials
  • Standard Operating Procedures: Clear guidelines for information processing
  • Performance Monitoring: Regular assessment of RTI implementation
  • Best Practice Sharing: Learning from successful state models

Future Directions

Legislative Reforms

  • Review of exemption clauses for relevance
  • Strengthening penalty provisions
  • Protection for RTI activists
  • Whistleblower protection mechanisms

Institutional Strengthening

  • Independence of Information Commissions
  • Adequate staffing and resources
  • Regular performance audits
  • Technology integration
